Module 05 · Plan vs. actual

Variance Analysis.

Reality almost never matches the budget. The question is why. Variance analysis is the discipline of decomposing the gap between planned and actual margin into its sources — Volume, Mix, Yield and Price — so management knows where to act.

1 · The control loop

From budget to action.

Variance analysis sits at the bottom of the planning–controlling cycle. It compares actuals to budget, decomposes the gap, and feeds the result back into next year's plan — and into the decisions managers take this quarter.

2 · The decomposition

The VMYP method.

A single number — "we missed the budget by €113" — is useless for action. The VMYP method splits the total variance into four economically distinct sub-variances, each pointing to a different manager and a different lever.

V
Volume
Did we sell more or fewer units than planned? Owned by Sales.
M
Mix
Did we sell a different combination of products? Owned by Sales / Product.
Y
Yield
Did we use more or less material per unit? Owned by Operations.
P
Price / Cost
Did we charge or pay a different price than budgeted? Owned by Sales / Procurement.
Core identity
Total variance = sum of all sub-variances
The VMYP method uses flexible budgets as intermediaries — successive recomputations of the budget that change one assumption at a time, isolating the effect of each. Done correctly, the sub-variances must add up to the total.
3 · A small but important convention

Favourable vs. unfavourable.

Variances are signed. The convention is universal: anything that increases the production margin compared to plan is favourable; anything that decreases it is unfavourable. This is true regardless of whether the underlying line is a revenue or a cost.

Favourable
  • Higher sales volume than plan
  • Higher selling price than plan
  • Lower material consumption per unit
  • Lower input prices than plan
  • Lower fixed costs than plan
Unfavourable
  • Lower sales volume than plan
  • Lower selling price than plan
  • Higher material consumption per unit
  • Higher input prices than plan
  • Higher fixed costs than plan
4 · A complete worked example

The flexible budget cascade.

The classic textbook example. A firm planned to sell 100 units at €50; it actually sold 115 at €48. Material yield improved (3.20 → 3.10 kg/unit), material price worsened (€8.80 → €8.90 / kg), and fixed costs overshot by €50. Total margin improved by €113 — but where did it come from?

The headline "+€113" was hiding a more revealing story: Sales drove +€328 by selling 15 extra units, and Operations contributed +€101 in yield. Together they more than offset a €230 hit from a lower-than-planned selling price, plus smaller hits on material price and fixed costs. The diagnosis points to pricing discipline as the area for management attention — not volume or efficiency, which are doing their job.

6 · The honest case

What VMYP does and doesn't do.

Benefits
  • Identifies the specific source of every euro of margin gap.
  • Routes responsibility to the right manager — Sales, Operations, or Procurement.
  • Protects against false alarms when volume changes drive cost variances.
  • Builds organisational learning that feeds the next planning cycle.
Limitations
  • The result depends on the order in which assumptions are flexed.
  • Backward-looking by nature — explains the past, doesn't predict the future.
  • Useless if the original budget itself was wrong — garbage in, garbage out.
  • Can incentivise gaming: managers pad the budget so the variance is "favourable".
7 · Looking ahead

The role of AI in management control.

Most of variance analysis is mechanical: pulling actuals, building flexible budgets, computing sub-variances, drafting commentary. This is exactly the kind of work modern AI tools are good at — but the judgement layer (which variance matters? who do we hold accountable? what do we change?) is not automatable. The right model is human-in-the-loop.

8 · Test your understanding

Three short questions.

In the worked example, the selling price variance is the largest unfavourable item. Whose performance does it speak to first?
A €2 drop in unit price across 115 units = €230 of margin lost. That's a pricing discussion, not an operations or procurement one.
A flexible budget at actual volume is most useful when you want to…
The whole purpose of flexing to actual volume is to remove the volume effect, so the remaining variance is "pure" price/efficiency.
Which statement about variance analysis is most accurate?
Variance analysis is fundamentally backward-looking — its value is in the lessons it generates, not in any predictive guarantee.
