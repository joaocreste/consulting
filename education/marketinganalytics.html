Module I · Decision-Driven Thinking

Information → insight → decision.

Analytics that does not change a decision is journalism. Module I introduces the logic that runs through the entire course: name the decision, lay out the choices, attach probabilities and payoffs, and choose the action with the highest expected value — adjusted for what the data does not tell you.

Most managers reach for analysis the way a tourist reaches for a phrasebook — urgently, after the conversation has already started. Decision-driven thinking inverts the order. You start with the decision you face, sketch the choices you have, and only then ask: what would I need to know to choose between them?

The discipline borrows three tools from operations research: decision trees (a graphical map of choices and chance), expected value (the probability-weighted average of outcomes), and the value of perfect information (how much you'd pay to remove an uncertainty). Together they let you reason about ambiguous commercial decisions the same way an underwriter reasons about risk.

Concept I
Decision Trees
A tree alternates decision nodes (squares — you choose) with chance nodes (circles — nature chooses). Each terminal leaf carries a payoff. Roll back from right to left, taking expected values at chance nodes and maximums at decision nodes.
Concept II
Expected Value
A weighted average of payoffs over states of the world. Honest only when probabilities are honest.
EV = Σi pi · vi     where Σpi = 1
Concept III
Value of Information
The difference between the expected value if you knew the truth in advance and the expected value of acting under uncertainty. It is the most you should pay for a study, a forecast, or a delay.
EVPI = EV(perfect info) − EV(best action now)
Concept IV
Risk Preference
EV is a guide, not a verdict. A risk-averse owner may prefer a smaller certain payoff to a larger expected one. Apply utility weights, or simply ask: could the company survive the worst branch?
Concept V
Sensitivity Testing
Vary each input across a plausible range. The decision is robust if the recommended action does not flip. The decision is fragile if a small probability change reverses the answer — flag fragility, do not hide it.
Concept VI
Option Value
Some decisions can be deferred. Waiting itself has value — you preserve optionality and learn from new information. The harvest decision in Freemark Abbey is, structurally, an option to wait.
Case I · Worked Solution
Freemark Abbey Winery
SettingNapa Valley · September 1976
ProtagonistWilliam Jaeger, Partner
Production~1,000 cases Riesling · 12,000 bottles
DecisionHarvest now, or wait through the storm
Setup
A premium winery, a forecast storm, and a fork in the vineyard.

William Jaeger's Riesling grapes are nearly ready. A weather report puts the probability of a rainstorm reaching Napa at 50%. If the storm hits and the weather is warm, Botrytis cinerea — the noble rot — can form, concentrating sugars and producing a luscious sweet wine that wholesales at $8.00/bottle. The mold reduces juice yield by 30%. If the storm hits but the rot does not form, the rainwater simply dilutes the berries and the wine sells for a thin $2.00/bottle.

If Jaeger harvests now, he sidesteps the storm entirely and bottles a dry Riesling that wholesales at $2.85/bottle — modest, but certain. If he waits and the storm misses, three sub-states are possible: a rich vintage at $3.50, a lighter one at $3.00, or a low-acid year at $2.50.

Sensitivity
When does the recommendation flip?

Vary the inputs that matter most: the storm probability and the botrytis probability conditional on the storm.

  • If P(botrytis | storm) falls from 0.40 to 0.18, EV(Wait) drops below $34,200. Below ~18% rot probability, harvest now.
  • If Jaeger could dump diluted wine at half price ($1.00/bottle = $12,000), the storm-no-rot branch falls and EV(Wait) becomes $35,640 — still above harvest, but only barely.
  • If risk aversion is binding — for instance, the worst branch ($24,000) would breach a debt covenant — the certainty premium of harvesting now justifies forgoing $5,040 in expectation.
Recommendation
Wait — but only if a $24,000 outcome is survivable.
Expected value points to waiting: $39,240 vs $34,200, a 14.7% uplift. The recommendation is, however, fragile — it depends on Jaeger's belief that P(botrytis | storm) is closer to 40% than to 20%, and on whether the winery can absorb the 30% probability of $24,000 thin wine without distressing operations. If those conditions hold, plant the option to wait. If not, harvest now and accept the $5,040 certainty premium as the price of sleeping at night.

Module II · The Analytics Spectrum

Four kinds of question, in strict order.

Most analytics projects fail on the brief, not the model. Module II teaches problem framing as a craft — distinguishing decisions in disguise from genuine uncertainties, choosing an objective function on purpose, and recognising that the most important variable in any model is the one chosen for the left-hand side.

I
DESCRIPTIVE
What happened? Counts, distributions, comparisons over time. Often where the question hides.
II
DIAGNOSTIC
Why did it happen? Decompose, segment, and isolate the drivers using comparisons and slicing.
III
PREDICTIVE
What is likely next? Estimate a model that maps inputs to outcomes and reduces uncertainty.
IV
PRESCRIPTIVE
So what — what should we do? Optimise an objective subject to constraints; this is where decisions live.
Concept · Information matrix
What you need to know vs what you know
Crossing need-to-know with know yields four quadrants: Foundational (maintain), Missing (seek — the priority), Random (ignore), Meaningless (stop). The whole discovery brief should aim at the Missing quadrant.
Concept · Causal direction
Whichever variable is on the left, you have assumed the cause
Regress price on demand and you have implicitly modelled price as caused by demand. Regress demand on price and you have inverted the causal arrow. The same data, two different worlds. Choose deliberately.
demand = f(price, …) ≠ price = f(demand, …)
Concept · Optimisation
Decision variables, objective, constraints
Every prescriptive model has three pieces: the levers you control, the metric you maximise, and the lines you will not cross. Most arguments inside firms are arguments about the second piece — which objective.
max f(x) s.t. g(x) ≤ b x ≥ 0

Module III · Customer Value & Targeting

Not every customer is worth the same coupon.

The average treatment effect tells you whether a campaign worked on average. It hides what every marketer secretly suspects: most of the lift came from a few segments, and other segments were probably hurt. Module III teaches Customer Lifetime Value as a value scale and uplift modelling as a targeting policy.

CLV · Method 1
Discounted individual transactions
Sum the discounted gross profit of every transaction a customer has made. Average across customers in a segment.
CLVi = Σj=1..m uij / (1+r)tij CLV̄ = (1/N) · Σi CLVi
Worked example: Costco customer P91, 56 transactions over 5.66 years, r = 10%, gives CLV(P91) = $3,180.11. Segment averages: Personal $2,099, Business $13,558.
CLV · Method 2a
Aggregate, varying retention, finite horizon
When you don't have individual transaction logs but you do have year-by-year gross profit and retention rates.
CLV̄ = ΣT=0..H uT · ∏t<T RRt,t+1 · (1+i)−T
Personal CLV (10y) = $2,094.47 · Business CLV (10y) = $12,637.77.
CLV · Method 2b · ∞
Closed-form, constant retention & profit
The shorthand most marketing decks quote. Constant margin m, retention r, discount rate d:
CLV (∞) = m · (1+d) / (1+d − r) ≈ m · r / (1+d − r) ← future-only
Personal: $631.68 × 1.10 / (1.10 − 0.77) = $2,105.60. Business: $2,301.11 × 1.10 / (1.10 − 0.91) = $13,322.22. All three methods converge — the choice is governed by data availability and the steadiness of the customer relationship.
Concept · Uplift
ATE, CATE, and the targeting score
The Average Treatment Effect is a single number across the test population. The Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE) is the lift for customers like this one — by channel, cart status, recency. Score every prospect, rank by predicted CATE, target only those above zero.
ATE = E[Y | T=1] − E[Y | T=0] CATE(x) = E[Y | T=1, X=x] − E[Y | T=0, X=x]

Headline numbers — A/B test
The average treatment effect is a yes and a no.

Read on conversion alone, the coupon worked. Read on revenue, the same coupon was value-destructive — every treated customer spent 24 cents less than they would have in control. The 20% discount on the marginal converter ate the lift. Blanket-targeting fails by exactly the amount the discount cannibalises the customers who would have bought anyway.

Heterogeneity
The lift is not flat. It is hidden inside two segments.

The pattern is structural, not noise. Customers who left a product in their cart and arrived through Facebook, Instagram or Referral are the coupon's responders. Customers acquired via Google or "Other" — who generally spend more at full price — do not need the coupon, and the discount subtracts from revenue. Those who never opened a cart are simply not engaged enough for a coupon to matter.

Targeting policy
Score the next 6,000, send the coupon to about a third.

Build an uplift model on the AB_Test sample (e.g. two outcome models for treated and control, or a meta-learner) that predicts the incremental revenue for each prospect given their features. Apply the model to the 6,000 users in Next_Campaign. Rank by predicted uplift. Send coupons only where predicted uplift > 0.

Empirical thresholds from the AB_Test pattern: include if shopping_cart = 1, channel_acq ∈ {2, 3, 4}, and weeks_since_visit ≤ 3. Roughly 30–35% of the next 6,000 will pass — the rest get nothing (or a non-discount touch).

Recommendation
Move from blanket to selective couponing.
A targeted policy turns Artea's revenue ATE from −$0.24/customer to a positive ~+$1.20/customer on those treated. Targeting ~30% of the file means total campaign revenue rises while discount cost falls two-thirds. The caveat: repeated coupon use trains customers to wait for discounts. Cap frequency, vary the offer, and re-test the policy quarterly.
Did the coupon work?
On conversion: +1.7 pp across the board. On revenue: negative on average, positive on the right segments. Both statements are true; reporting only one is malpractice.
Should Artea continue couponing?
Yes — but selectively, on customers with active carts and the right acquisition channel, capped in frequency to avoid eroding price expectation.
By how much can revenue rise?
If targeted at the cart=1 + channels {2,3,4} sub-population (~30%), revenue per treated customer should run ~$1.20–$1.40 above control, with discount cost ~⅓ of the blanket campaign.
Module IV · Digital Analytics

A 7:1 ROAS can be a fiction.

Digital marketing produces dashboards faster than it produces understanding. Module IV separates four kinds of confusion that look identical on a chart but lead to opposite decisions: wrong audience, wrong metric, wrong attribution, wrong allocation.

Concept · Funnel
AIDA
Awareness → Interest → Desire → Action. Lower-funnel spend (Action) needs enough conversions to train the bidding algorithm. A "Book Now" creative on a tiny audience has neither.
Concept · Attribution
Last-click vs incrementality
Last-click credits the final touch with 100% of the conversion. Incrementality asks: would the conversion have happened anyway? Only the second metric is decision-grade.
Concept · Equimarginal principle
Allocate until marginal returns equalise
The next pound goes to the channel with the highest marginal return — not the highest average. Stop when marginal returns equalise across channels.
∂R/∂xk equal across k (and = price of capital, if budgets are flexible)
Concept · Matched-market test
Geo-experiments
Pick paired markets matched on baseline volume; turn the channel off in one, leave it on in the other; compare. Gold-standard incrementality test for channels that resist user-level holdouts (e.g. branded paid search).
Concept · Average vs marginal ROI
Two different questions
Average ROI ranks historical winners. Marginal ROI tells you where the next dollar belongs. They are different and they often disagree.
Concept · CLV as the value metric
Volume vs Value
A campaign that produces more accounts at lower CLV produces less business value. Pick the metric that survives a 12-month horizon.

Module V · ML, MMM & the Accountability Gap

When the algorithm meets the P&L.

The final module is about putting an analytical model into a global organisation. The technique is Marketing Mix Modelling — adstock, saturation, response curves. The harder problem is human: brand directors who do not want their pet channel cut, sales reps who do not want their territory algorithm-managed, finance teams sceptical that a model can move a P&L.

MMM · Adstock
Advertising has memory
A pound of TV spent today still drives sales next week. Adstock encodes that memory as an exponential decay.
At = xt + λ · At−1 0 ≤ λ < 1
λ near zero ≈ short-lived performance media; λ near one ≈ long-half-life brand media.
MMM · Saturation
Diminishing returns are real
Doubling spend never doubles response. Saturation curves bend.
R(x) = α · x / (β + x) Hill / Michaelis-Menten R(x) = α · log(1 + x/β) log-saturation
MMM · Estimation
Bounded Variable Least Squares
BVLS constrains coefficients to non-negative for media (advertising shouldn't reduce sales) and negative for price (a price cut should lift volume). It stabilises a model where multicollinearity is the rule, not the exception.
MMM · Decomposition
Baseline vs incremental
A fitted MMM splits weekly volume into baseline (intercept + trend + seasonality + price) and incremental (each channel's adstocked, saturated contribution). The baseline is brand equity; the incremental is the marginal media pound.
Pernod Ricard · the four KDPs
In-house digital programs, scaled across 70+ affiliates.

In April 2020, Pernod Ricard's first Chief Digital Officer, Pierre-Yves Calloc'h, selected four Key Digital Programs from a shortlist of twenty. Built in-house — because off-the-shelf would have meant exposing 50 strategic business parameters per program — the four mature programs each target a different commercial decision.

KDP · Matrix
Marketing Mix Modelling
Allocates marketing budget across brands and channels (TV, digital, social, in-store activation). Trained on three years of weekly sales + marketing data. Produces response curves and a recommended allocation. Adoption 60–70%. Reported impact: up to 15% gain in marketing efficiency.
KDP · D-STAR
Sales-rep recommendations
Clusters retail outlets using 30–50 features (size, brands sold, demographics, proximity to bars/leisure). Each Monday gives reps a recommended visit list with tailored actions: negotiate a new SKU, increase order size, push a promotion. Pilot in France: +1.2% net sales vs control.
KDP · Vista Rev-up
Promotion timing
Scenario simulation for optimal promotional windows. Less mature than Matrix and D-STAR; not yet at full deployment.
KDP · Maestria
Consumer-choice prediction
ML to forecast consumer preference at category and SKU level. Earlier in the maturity curve; intended to feed Matrix and Vista Rev-up.

Findings

Finding I
Out-of-Home is the most efficient channel by a factor of two.
OOH delivers 3.08 incremental units per £1k spent — more than double the next best channel. Yet it absorbs only 4.7% of media budget, while contributing 6.6% of total volume. The model says: lean in.
Finding II

More than half of weekly volume would persist with zero media spend, driven by trend, seasonality and Christmas. The brand is not being built from scratch each week — but the marginal pound still shapes 42% of outcomes.
