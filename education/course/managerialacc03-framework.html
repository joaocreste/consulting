Module 03 · From strategy to measurement

Management Control
Framework.

A great strategy means nothing if you can't tell whether you're getting there. This module turns abstract strategic intent into measurable performance — through SMART objectives, the Balanced Scorecard, the OVAR method, dashboards, and KPIs.

1 · Setting the destination

What is a strategic objective?

Strategic objectives are the long-term targets that translate a company's mission and vision into something the organisation can actually pursue. They are the bridge between "what we believe in" and "what we will do this quarter."

Definition
Strategic objective
A specific, long-term target that translates an organisation's mission, vision and overall strategy into measurable goals — concrete enough to be planned for, resourced, and ultimately controlled.
01
Mission
Why we exist. The reason the firm is in business at all.
02
Vision
Where we want to be. The future we are aiming at.
03
Strategic objectives
The specific long-term targets that will get us there.
04
Action plans
The concrete projects, budgets and KPIs that operationalise the objectives.
2 · Making objectives operational

The SMART test.

A strategic objective only earns the name once it can pass five basic tests. SMART is the discipline that prevents a wishy-washy "improve customer experience" from masquerading as a real objective.

S
Specific
Defines exactly what is to be achieved — no ambiguity, no hedging.
M
Measurable
Includes a quantifiable indicator. If you can't count it, you can't control it.
A
Achievable
Realistic given current resources, capabilities and constraints.
R
Relevant
Aligned with the broader mission, vision and strategy of the firm.
T
Time-bound
Has a deadline. Open-ended objectives are never delivered.
Worked example · Unilever
"Reduce greenhouse-gas emissions per consumer by 50% by 2030."
Specific (GHG per consumer), Measurable (50%), Achievable (consistent with industry trajectories), Relevant (climate-aligned strategy) and Time-bound (2030). A textbook SMART objective.
Worked example · Tesla
"Deliver 20 million vehicles annually by 2030."
Specific volume target, measurable, time-bound and clearly relevant to a strategy of mass-market electrification. Whether it is achievable is exactly the kind of question that management control is built to surface — quarter by quarter.
3 · The classic framework

The Balanced Scorecard.

Introduced by Kaplan and Norton in 1992, the Balanced Scorecard (BSC) refuses the idea that financial results alone tell you how the business is doing. It demands you measure performance from four perspectives — and trace the cause-and-effect links between them.

The four perspectives form a logical chain: invest in your people and systems, that improves internal processes, that creates value for customers, that ultimately drives financial performance. The arrows of cause-and-effect run upward through the scorecard — which is why it is sometimes drawn as a strategy map.

Updated BSC · The fifth perspective
ESG & sustainability
Modern Balanced Scorecards add a fifth perspective for environmental and social outcomes — emissions, diversity, governance — recognising that long-term financial value depends on managing impacts that sit outside the original four lenses. We treat this in detail in module 06.
4 · The French alternative

The OVAR method.

OVAR (Objectifs · Variables d'Action · Responsables) — also called O/CPV (Objectives, Critical Performance Variables) — is the French school's answer to the BSC. Where the Balanced Scorecard organises measures by perspective, OVAR organises them by action levers and assigns each lever to a named owner.

Objectives
  • Clarify where the organisation wants to go.
  • Long-term and tied to the strategy.
  • Each one becomes the column header in the OVAR grid.
Critical Performance Variables (CPVs)
  • The action levers that move the objectives.
  • Indicate how to support strategic goals.
  • Each one becomes a row, owned by a named manager.

Example · E-Commerce Co.

Two strategic objectives. Four critical performance variables. The grid makes the linkage explicit — and makes it impossible to claim a CPV without specifying what objective it serves.

Critical Performance Variable O1 · Grow revenue 20% O2 · Improve customer loyalty Owner
CPV1 · Website conversion rate ●●● ●● Head of Digital
CPV2 · Average order value ●●● ● Head of Merchandising
CPV3 · On-time delivery rate ● ●●● Head of Operations
CPV4 · Customer service NPS ● ●●● Head of Customer Care

●●● strong driver · ●● moderate · ● weak. Reading down a column tells you which levers move that objective. Reading across a row tells the owner what they are accountable for.

BSC vs OVAR
Two ways to translate strategy into measurement
The Balanced Scorecard organises measures around four perspectives and constructs a strategy map showing cause-and-effect. OVAR organises measures around action levers and constructs a grid assigning each lever to a responsible manager. BSC is stronger on narrative and balance; OVAR is stronger on accountability and operational granularity.
5 · Putting measures in front of managers

The dashboard.

A dashboard is the graphical user interface of management control — the at-a-glance view of the KPIs that matter for a particular objective or business process. Done well, a dashboard mixes financial and non-financial indicators so that decisions are made with the full picture, not just the P&L.

Definition
Dashboard
A graphical interface that provides at-a-glance views of the key performance indicators relevant to a specific objective or process. It aims to control a coherent set of KPIs that match every dimension of the underlying performance model — both objectives and actions.

Monitoring versus Energizing indicators

Not every KPI is on a dashboard for the same reason. Some exist to keep the wheels on the bus (monitoring); others exist to push the organisation toward its strategic ambitions (energizing). Healthy dashboards include both.

Type Monitoring indicators Energizing indicators
Purpose Detect deviations from normal operations. Push behaviour toward strategic priorities.
Time horizon Short term, high frequency. Medium to long term, lower frequency.
Tone Defensive — "is anything broken?" Offensive — "are we moving the needle?"
Examples Cash balance, stock-out rate, on-time delivery. Innovation pipeline, ESG score, market-share gain.

Three steps to design one

01
Identify objectives
Start from the strategy. What outcomes is this dashboard trying to drive?
02
Map action levers
Use BSC perspectives or an OVAR grid to surface the variables that move those outcomes.
03
Pick KPIs
Translate each lever into one or two indicators. Resist the urge to measure everything.
6 · The unit of measurement

What is a KPI?

A KPI — Key Performance Indicator — is a quantifiable measure used to evaluate success against a specific objective. The word key matters: a metric only earns the K once it is genuinely decision-grade. Most "KPIs" you'll see in the wild are just metrics with delusions of grandeur.

Definition
Key Performance Indicator (KPI)
A quantifiable measure used to evaluate the success of an organisation, employee or activity in meeting performance objectives. KPIs must accurately match the dimensions of the underlying performance model — both objectives and actions.

Examples by perspective

Perspective Sample KPIs
Financial Revenue growth · Operating margin · Return on invested capital · Free cash flow · EVA
Customer Net Promoter Score · Customer retention rate · Market share · Average order value · CSAT
Internal Processes Cycle time · Defect rate · On-time delivery · Cost per unit · Capacity utilisation
Learning & Growth Training hours per employee · Employee engagement · Internal mobility · Time-to-productivity
ESG (5th) Scope 1 & 2 emissions · Diversity ratios · Board independence · Supplier audit coverage

8 · Test your understanding

Three short questions.

Which is the third perspective of the original Balanced Scorecard, after Financial and Customer?
The original four are: Financial, Customer, Internal Processes, and Learning & Growth. ESG is a modern fifth-perspective extension.
In the OVAR method, what does "CPV" stand for?
CPVs are the action levers (variables d'action) that drive each strategic objective and are owned by a named manager.
"Improve customer experience" is not a SMART objective. Which letter does it most clearly fail?
Without a quantified target (NPS ≥ X, retention ≥ Y%) and a deadline, "improve customer experience" can't be tracked. It fails M (measurable) and T (time-bound).
