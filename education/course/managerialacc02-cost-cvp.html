Module 02 · Cost behaviour

Cost & CVP
Analysis.

The intuition is simple: every product carries some cost that scales with volume, and some that does not. Once you separate the two, you can answer the questions that actually matter — break-even, target profit, the price floor, and the consequence of every operating decision.

1 · The vocabulary of cost

What is a cost?

Cost is the monetary value of the resources sacrificed to achieve a specific objective. Two further distinctions are worth fixing in mind from the start.

Definition
Actual vs. budgeted cost
Actual cost is a cost that has occurred. Budgeted cost is a predicted cost. Both are essential — actuals to know what happened, budgets to plan and to compare against.
Definition
Cost object
Anything for which a cost measurement is desired — a product, a service, a project, a customer, an activity, a department, even a single transaction.
Cost objectIllustration
ProductA bicycle
ServiceAn evaluation of damages after a disaster
ProjectAn airplane assembled by Airbus for British Airways
CustomerA dealer that purchases a broad range of BMW vehicles
ActivitySetting up machines for production · quality testing
DepartmentHR · Environment, Health & Safety

2 · Three useful classifications

How costs are classified.

Costs can be sorted in many ways, but three classifications dominate practice:

A · By behaviour
Fixed vs. variable
Does the cost change with the level of activity (output, units sold)? Critical for CVP and break-even analysis.
B · By assignment
Direct vs. indirect
Can the cost be traced specifically to a single cost object, or is it shared across many?
C · By function
Manufacturing vs. non-manufacturing
Production costs (raw material, direct labour, overhead) vs. period costs (marketing, distribution, customer service, legal, admin).

3 · Behaviour vs. volume

Fixed costs and variable costs.

Fixed Costs (FC)
  • Remain unchanged within the relevant range, regardless of activity
  • Examples: rent of buildings, supervisor salaries, insurance, plant leasing
  • Total FC stays flat as output grows
  • But FC per unit falls as output grows (the “spread” effect)
Variable Costs (VC)
  • Change in proportion to changes in the level of activity
  • Examples: raw material, piece-rate labour, transport per shipment
  • Total VC rises linearly with output
  • But VC per unit stays constant across the relevant range

Total cost

Combine the two and you get the most basic equation in cost accounting:

TC = FC + VC TC = FC + (VCu × Q) TR = SP × Q

Where TC = total cost · FC = fixed cost · VCu = variable cost per unit · Q = quantity · TR = total revenue · SP = selling price per unit.

4 · A short case

How basic cost analysis can change a strategy — Xerox 914.

In 1959 Xerox launched the 914 photocopier. The business problem was painful: production cost was $2,000 per unit, while the market expected to buy copiers at $300–$400. Major customers (IBM, GE, Kodak) saw no future in the product:

IBM’s verdict, 1959
“Although it may be admirably suited for a few specialized copying applications, the Model 914 has no future in the office-copying-equipment market.”
A market signal that, in retrospect, was wrong — but only because Xerox changed the business model, not the product.

The pivot

Rather than sell the machine, Xerox leased it as a service:

For Xerox
  • Redesigned components for compatibility (90 % reuse)
  • Doubled labour cost for cleaning, repairing, replacing
  • Recovered 24,000 t of components — generating $200 m extra profit by 1999
For the client
  • $95 per month up to 2,000 copies
  • $0.04 per additional copy after 2,000
  • Cleaning, repair, replacement included
  • Fixed cost transformed into variable cost
  • 15-day cancellation window — easy to scale

One simple cost-classification insight — that customers were unwilling to absorb a fixed cost they could not match to actual usage — completely re-shaped the business model. The product never changed.

5 · The core technique

Cost-Volume-Profit (CVP) analysis.

CVP analysis is the study of how changes in costs and volume affect a company’s profit. It is one of the most useful tools in management accounting because it answers concrete questions:

  • How many units must we sell to break even?
  • How many to hit a target profit?
  • What happens to profit if the selling price moves?
  • What happens if a chunk of fixed cost can be removed?
  • Which product mix is most profitable given a constraint?

Limitations to keep in mind

CVP rests on assumptions that are convenient but rarely fully true: cost and revenue behaviour is linear within the relevant range; costs split cleanly into fixed and variable; activity is the only driver of cost; and all units produced are sold. Use CVP for clear-headed decision support — but remember which assumptions you are leaning on.

6 · The key concept

The contribution margin.

Definition
Contribution margin (CM)
The amount of revenue remaining after deducting variable costs. It is what is left from sales (1) to cover fixed costs and (2) to generate profit.
CM = TR − VC CMu = SP − VCu CM ratio = CMu ÷ SP × 100%

The CVP income statement

Unlike the financial accounting income statement, the CVP version reports the contribution margin explicitly — separating variable from fixed costs. Net income comes out the same; the structure makes decision support possible.

CVP Income StatementTotal ($)Per unit ($)
Sales800,000500
Variable expenses (COGS, selling, admin)480,000300
Contribution Margin320,000200
Fixed expenses200,000
Net Income120,000

CM = 800,000 − 480,000 = 320,000 · CM ratio = 200 / 500 = 40 % · 40 % of every revenue dollar is available to cover fixed costs and add to profit.

7 · The fundamental question

The break-even point.

Definition
Break-even point (BEP)
The level of activity at which profit equals zero — total revenue exactly covers total cost (both fixed and variable). It can be expressed in units or in sales value.

Three ways to reach the same number

You can derive the break-even point three ways. They are mathematically equivalent.

i · Equation method
Set TC = TR
FC + (VCu × Q*) = SP × Q* → Q* = FC ÷ (SP − VCu) = FC ÷ CMu
ii · Contribution-margin method
Cover fixed costs with CM
FC ÷ CMu = BEP in units · FC ÷ CM ratio = BEP in sales value
iii · The CVP graph
Where TR crosses TC
Plot total revenue and total cost against quantity — the BEP is the intersection point.

8 · A worked example

The NYC–Paris flight.

An airline operates a single flight from New York to Paris with the following economics:

ItemValue
Average ticket price$400
Fixed cost of each flight$100,000
Variable cost per passenger$50

Question: what is the minimum number of passengers needed not to lose money on this flight?

CMu = 400 − 50 = 350 BEP = FC ÷ CMu = 100,000 ÷ 350 = 285.7

The flight becomes profitable from 286 passengers upwards. Expressed in sales value: 285.7 × $400 ≈ $114,280.

10 · Beyond break-even

From break-even to a target net income.

The same logic that got you to zero profit gets you to any target profit — just include the desired profit in the numerator:

Q = (FC + Desired profit) ÷ CMu Sales = (FC + Desired profit) ÷ CM ratio

Example: with FC = $100,000, CMu = $350, and a target profit of $35,000, you need (100,000 + 35,000) / 350 = 386 passengers.

11 · Strategic implications

Why cost structure matters strategically.

Two firms with the same total revenue can react very differently to the same shock — because their cost structures are different. This is operating leverage.

High fixed costs · low variable costs
  • Each additional sale converts a high % of revenue into profit
  • Powerful when volumes are high
  • Painful when demand drops — fixed costs do not move
  • Strategy: focus on volume growth
Low fixed costs · high variable costs
  • Each sale contributes less to profit, but loss exposure is limited
  • Resilient when demand is volatile
  • Less upside when volume booms
  • Strategy: protect margin per unit

A 20 % drop in demand will hurt the high-fixed-cost firm more — most of its costs cannot shrink to match. This is exactly what struck premium-luxury and aviation businesses during COVID.

13 · Quick check

Test your understanding.

A company sells a product for $50. Variable cost per unit is $30 and total fixed costs are $20,000. How many units must be sold to break even?
CMu = $50 − $30 = $20. BEP = $20,000 ÷ $20 = 1,000 units. (Note: the choices are unforgiving — pick the one closest to the formula.)
Companies X and Y have the same total revenue, but X has higher fixed costs and lower variable costs, while Y has lower fixed costs and higher variable costs. If demand drops 20 %, which company is likely to experience the larger percentage decrease in profit?
Company X has higher operating leverage. Its profit is more sensitive to changes in sales because most of its costs cannot shrink with volume.
A firm outsources production to a third party instead of manufacturing in-house. What is the most likely impact on its cost structure?
Outsourcing eliminates internal capacity costs (rent, machinery, supervision) — so fixed costs fall. But each unit now bears a per-unit charge from the supplier — so variable costs rise. This is exactly the Xerox 914 transformation in reverse.
A company has two product launch options. Option 1: high fixed costs of $1,000,000 but low variable costs of $10/unit. Option 2: low fixed costs of $100,000 but high variable costs of $40/unit. If demand is expected to be high, which option is better?
When volume is high, the structure with the lowest variable cost wins — the contribution margin per unit is much higher, so each additional sale generates more profit. Option 1 has CMu advantages of $30 per unit.
