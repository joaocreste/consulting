02 · Findings

Four conclusions, drawn from the data alone.

Finding I
Out-of-Home is the most efficient channel by a factor of two.
OOH delivers 3.08 incremental units per £1k spent — more than double the next best channel. Yet it absorbs only 4.7% of media budget, while contributing 6.6% of total volume. The model says: lean in.
Finding II
Trade marketing carries the heaviest load.
On-Trade and Off-Trade together drive 20.8% of volume — more than any pair of above-the-line channels. For a vodka brand, the bar and the shelf still convert harder than the screen.
Finding III
Social spend shows no measurable lift.
With £8.6M deployed over the period, Social returns only 0.13 units per £1k — eight times less than On-Trade. Either the creative isn't converting, or the model attributes its lift elsewhere. Either way: review.
Finding IV
A 58% baseline points to genuine brand equity.
More than half of weekly volume would persist with zero media spend, driven by trend, seasonality, and Christmas. The brand is not being built from scratch each week — but the marginal pound still shapes 42% of outcomes.

09 · Methodology

How the model was built.

Sales volume was modelled as a linear combination of transformed media variables, a price index, and time-based controls. Media spend was first adstocked using a geometric decay (carryover) parameter optimised per channel by coordinate descent on R². The adstocked series was then saturated via log(1 + spend / scale), where scale is the channel's median weekly active spend. The full system was solved via Bounded Variable Least Squares (BVLS), constraining all media coefficients and the price-lift coefficient to be non-negative — preserving economic interpretability. Time controls (linear trend, Fourier seasonality at annual and semi-annual frequencies, and a Christmas/NYE indicator) were left unbounded.

Step 1
Adstock Transform
Geometric carryover per channel: x*(t) = spend(t) + λ · x*(t−1). Optimal λ ranged from 0.0 (Social) to 0.50 (OOH), reflecting how long each medium continues to influence purchase after exposure.
Step 2
Saturation Curve
Log(1 + x/scale) applied to adstocked spend, where scale = median nonzero weekly spend. Captures diminishing returns without imposing a steep Hill-function turning point.
Step 3
Bounded Regression
BVLS constrains media coefficients ≥ 0 — ruling out negative lift estimates that would be economically incoherent. Controls remain unbounded.
Step 4
Decomposition & Curves
Per-week contribution computed as coefficient × transformed spend. Response curves derived analytically from the steady-state form of adstock + saturation.

Limitations. The model assumes additive separability between channels — no explicit interaction effects. Adstock and saturation parameters are deterministic rather than Bayesian (no posterior credible intervals). Media spend may correlate with unobserved drivers such as competitor activity, distribution changes, or PR coverage. An 18% residual variance is unaccounted for. Findings are directionally robust; precise reallocation should be validated through controlled in-market tests.
