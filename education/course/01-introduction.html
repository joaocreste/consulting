Module 01 · Foundations

Introduction &
Foundations.

Before any calculator, formula, or scorecard, two questions decide everything: who is the information for? and what decision should it support? This module sets the language we will use across the rest of the course.

1 · The starting point

What is accounting?

Accounting is the process of identifying, measuring and communicating economic information to permit informed judgements and decisions by users of that information. So far, so neutral. The interesting question is which users — because that single choice splits the discipline in two.

Two audiences
External vs. internal users
External parties — investors, banks, governments — receive financial accounting: regulated, historical, aggregate. Internal parties — managers, teams, employees — receive management (or managerial) accounting: tailor-made, forward-looking, often non-financial as much as financial.

Accounting is not just the techniques. It is also a social system: the act of measuring something is also the act of declaring that it matters. What gets measured becomes recognised — and, eventually, gets managed.

2 · A first comparison

Financial vs. managerial accounting.

Both disciplines start from the same raw transactions. They diverge in audience, regulation, time horizon, level of detail, and the kind of measures they accept.

Financial accounting
  • Reports to parties outside the organization
  • Highly regulated (IFRS, GAAP)
  • Primarily historical (income statement, balance sheet, cash-flow)
  • External users: creditors, investors, suppliers, government
  • Financial measures only
  • Highly aggregate — reports on the entire organization
Managerial accounting
  • Deals with activities inside the organization
  • Unregulated — designed to be useful, not compliant
  • May use projections about the future
  • Internal users: shareholders, management, employees, partners
  • Tailor-made to internal needs
  • Financial and non-financial performance measures
  • Disaggregated to inform local decisions and actions

A useful shorthand: financial accounting tells the world where the company has been; managerial accounting helps the firm decide where it should go next.

3 · A subtle distinction

Managerial accounting ≠ management control.

The two terms are often used interchangeably, but it pays to keep them apart.

Managerial accounting
  • Provides the information — costs, margins, revenues, projections
  • Identifies, collects, classifies and reports data useful to managers
  • The raw material of decision-making
Management control
  • Uses that information to plan, execute and monitor the business
  • Ensures activities are aligned with strategic goals
  • The system in which decisions get made

Put simply: managerial accounting answers “what are the numbers?”; management control answers “what do we do with them?”

4 · The management cycle

Planning, controlling, and the loop between them.

All management activity oscillates between two complementary functions: planning (deciding what should happen) and controlling (checking what did happen and adjusting). Decision-making sits between them.

Planning

The detailed formulation of future actions to achieve a particular end. It requires setting objectives — short-term profit, environmental commitment, value-add, motivation — and identifying the methods to reach them.

Controlling

The ongoing managerial activity of monitoring a plan’s implementation and taking corrective action as needed. Control depends on feedback — the information used to evaluate or correct the steps being taken.

5 · From mission to action

The performance pyramid.

Strategy is useless if it does not translate into things people actually do on Tuesday morning. The performance pyramid is a discipline for cascading abstract intent into measurable, accountable action.

The cascade reads top-down: mission and vision inform goals, which produce objectives, which trigger initiatives, which are tracked by measures, which set the targets people are held to. Read bottom-up, the same pyramid is also how front-line evidence gets back to the strategy room.

6 · Why financials are not enough

Stakeholders, not just shareholders.

Revenue and costs tell you what happened. They do not tell you why it happened, whether it is sustainable, or how to improve it. A narrow financial focus risks short-term decision-making, missed growth opportunities, and ignored performance drivers.

Modern organisations exist to fulfil the demands of multiple stakeholder groups, and managers need to look beyond the income statement at customer experience, operational quality, innovation, and people.

StakeholderTypical expectations
ShareholdersProfitability, ROI, ROCE, ROE
CustomersTime, quality, price, services
EmployeesJob satisfaction, fair compensation, development
Suppliers & lendersReliability, payment discipline, transparency
SocietyEnvironment, ethics, community development

A guiding line for the rest of the course: financials tell the story, but management control explains and shapes it.

7 · Where accountability lives

The four responsibility centres.

A responsibility centre is an organisational unit whose manager is responsible for planning, controlling and decision-making within a defined scope, and is evaluated against specific performance metrics tied to that scope. The classification depends on what the manager actually controls.

01 · Cost centre
Cost efficiency
Manager is accountable for costs only — not for revenue. Typical example: a manufacturing plant or HR department. KPI: cost per unit, total spend vs. budget.
e.g. manufacturing · HR
02 · Revenue centre
Top-line generation
Manager is accountable for sales revenue but not the cost of producing what is sold. Typical example: a sales team. KPI: revenue, units sold, conversion rate.
e.g. sales team
03 · Profit centre
Revenue minus cost
Manager is accountable for both revenues and costs — but not for the capital invested. Typical example: a regional business unit. KPI: operating profit, contribution margin.
e.g. regional BU
04 · Investment centre
Profit and capital used
Manager is accountable for profits and the capital required to generate them. Typical example: an autonomous division or HQ. KPI: ROI, ROCE, EVA.
e.g. division · HQ

9 · Quick check

Test your understanding.

A regional sales office is judged on the revenue it generates, but not on the production cost of the goods it sells. What kind of responsibility centre is it?
A revenue centre is held accountable for top-line revenue but not for the cost of producing what is sold or the capital invested. That fits a sales office exactly.
Which statement best captures the relationship between managerial accounting and management control?
Managerial accounting is the information layer. Management control is the system that puts the information to work — through planning, execution, and monitoring against goals.
Which of the following is NOT a typical feature of managerial accounting?
Managerial accounting is unregulated — there is no IFRS or GAAP equivalent. That freedom is precisely what allows it to be tailored, forward-looking, and to mix financial with non-financial measures.
