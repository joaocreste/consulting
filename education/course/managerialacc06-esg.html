Module 06 · Beyond financials

ESG in Management
Control.

Financial performance is no longer enough. Regulators, investors and customers now hold firms to a broader standard — Environmental, Social and Governance. This module shows how ESG fits into the management control system and how to design ESG KPIs that survive scrutiny.

1 · The three letters

What does ESG mean?

ESG is a framework for evaluating a company along three dimensions that have historically sat outside the financial statements but are increasingly recognised as drivers — and risks — of long-term value.

E
Environmental
Climate change, energy use, GHG emissions, water, waste, pollution, biodiversity — the firm's footprint on the natural world.
S
Social
Employee wellbeing, diversity & inclusion, human rights in the supply chain, community impact, customer health and safety.
G
Governance
Board independence, executive pay, anti-corruption, business ethics, transparency, shareholder rights, audit quality.
2 · A common confusion

ESG vs. sustainability.

The two terms are often used interchangeably. They aren't the same. Sustainability is a goal; ESG is the measurement framework we use to track progress toward that goal — and to manage the financial risks of getting it wrong.

Sustainability
  • A long-term goal — meeting present needs without compromising future ones.
  • Holistic, value-driven, often qualitative.
  • Common languages of NGOs, policy-makers and corporate purpose statements.
  • Hard to operationalise on its own.
ESG
  • A measurement framework for non-financial performance and risk.
  • Structured, often quantitative, tied to disclosures and ratings.
  • Common language of investors, regulators and finance functions.
  • Designed to plug into management control systems.
3 · The pressure system

Why ESG matters for management control.

Three forces have moved ESG from "PR" to "P&L impact" — regulation, capital markets, and stakeholder expectations. Whether or not a firm cares about sustainability for its own sake, the management control system now has to track ESG metrics.

01
Regulation
CSRD & ESRS in Europe, SEC climate rules in the US, ISSB standards globally — disclosure is now mandatory for thousands of firms.
Disclosure
02
Capital markets
SFDR labels, MSCI & Sustainalytics ratings, green bonds — the cost of capital increasingly depends on ESG performance.
Cost of capital
03
Stakeholders
Customers, employees and communities now reward (and punish) firms based on ESG conduct — affecting revenue, talent and licence to operate.
Reputation & revenue
04
Frameworks
GRI, SASB, TCFD and ESRS provide the technical vocabulary and KPI definitions that management control teams need to measure consistently.
Standards
4 · How control evolves

Management control, updated.

The management control system inherited from the 20th century was built around financial inputs and outputs. The modern version expands the scope on both ends.

Traditional management control ESG-integrated management control
ScopeFinancial & operational performance only.Financial + environmental + social + governance.
Time horizonQuarterly & annual.Quarterly + multi-year & lifecycle.
AudienceShareholders & management.Stakeholders broadly — including regulators, customers, employees, communities.
IndicatorsRevenue, margin, ROIC, NPS.+ Scope 1/2/3 emissions, diversity, board independence, supplier audit coverage.
Data sourcesGeneral ledger, ERP.+ Energy meters, HR systems, supply-chain platforms, third-party assurance.
ReportingAnnual report, P&L.+ Sustainability report, CSRD disclosure, ratings submissions.
5 · Why it pays

ESG and long-term value.

ESG is not philanthropy. The link between ESG and shareholder value is now well-documented across the academic and practitioner literature: better ESG performers tend to face lower regulatory risk, attract and retain better talent, secure cheaper capital, and build more resilient supply chains.

Dimension Mechanism Where the value shows up
EnvironmentalResource efficiency, lower emissions, climate-risk management.Lower input costs · Lower regulatory penalties · Insurance.
SocialEngaged workforce, safer products, stronger community licence.Higher productivity · Lower turnover · Better brand.
GovernanceStrong oversight, low-corruption culture, transparent reporting.Lower cost of capital · Fewer scandals · Better access to markets.
6 · Quality criteria

What makes a good ESG KPI?

Most "ESG KPIs" you encounter in the wild fail at least three of these tests. A KPI that fails them isn't useless — it's worse than useless, because it gives the appearance of control without the substance. Seven criteria to apply to every metric you propose.

Criterion What it means
Strategic relevanceLinked to the company's core business model and long-term goals.
MaterialityFocused on ESG issues that matter most to the business and its stakeholders.
MeasurabilityBased on clear, reliable, consistent data — auditable, not estimated.
ActionabilityHelps managers make decisions and take concrete action.
ComparabilityBenchmarkable over time, and against peers, using a standard methodology.
Responsibility assignedOwned by a specific function or business unit — never floating.
BalancedIncludes both leading (predictive) and lagging (result) indicators.
7 · The build process

Six steps to design an ESG KPI.

A repeatable workflow for moving from a strategic intention to a measurable, owned, time-bound metric. Use this whenever the management control team is asked to add an ESG dimension to the existing scorecard.

01
Identify ESG priorities
Materiality assessment by sector. Manufacturing → CO₂, safety, waste. Tech → privacy, diversity. Finance → governance, responsible investing.
02
Link to strategic objectives
Each priority must map back to a strategic objective from module 03. If it doesn't, it doesn't belong on the scorecard.
03
Define SMART KPIs
"Reduce water usage in manufacturing by 20% by 2026." Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound.
04
Assign ownership
Tie each KPI to a responsibility centre. HR owns gender diversity in leadership; Ops owns energy per unit; Facilities owns water savings.
05
Set baseline & targets
Use benchmarks or recognised frameworks. SBTi for emissions targets, GRI for disclosures, sector medians for relative measures.
06
Monitor, report, improve
Track on the dashboard, report externally per CSRD/GRI/SASB, and feed lessons back into next year's planning cycle.

9 · Test your understanding

Three short questions.

Which statement most accurately captures the relationship between ESG and sustainability?
Sustainability is the long-term goal — meeting present needs without compromising future ones. ESG is the structured measurement framework that makes that goal manageable inside a control system.
Which of the following ESG KPIs would most clearly fail the "measurability" criterion?
No data, no number, no deadline. "Be a force for good" is a value statement, not a KPI — it fails measurability and time-bound criteria.
Why do many firms add a fifth perspective to the original Balanced Scorecard?
The fifth perspective addresses a real gap: emissions, diversity, governance and supply-chain risks materially affect future financial performance, but they don't sit cleanly inside Customer or Internal Processes.
Wrap-up

That's the course.

Six modules. The arc started with definitions and the planning–controlling loop, moved through cost behaviour and CVP, then translated strategy into measurement via the BSC and OVAR, learned to plan via budgets, learned to control via variance analysis, and finally widened the scope to ESG.

The thread running through all of it: management control is what turns a number on a page into a decision in a meeting. Innovation, taken further.
