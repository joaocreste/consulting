Module 04 · From plan to numbers

Planning &
Budgeting.

A budget is the financial expression of a plan. It is also a contract, a coordination device, a motivational tool — and, if mishandled, a generator of dysfunction. This module unpacks the six roles a budget plays and the seven different control approaches firms use in practice.

1 · The starting point

What is a budget?

Budgeting is the formal process of translating an organisation's strategy into quantified financial and operational targets for a future period — typically a year, broken down into months or quarters. It is the first half of the planning–controlling loop: budgets define what "good" looks like, so that variance analysis can later detect where reality diverged.

Definition
Budget
A quantitative plan, expressed in monetary and operational terms, that specifies what an organisation intends to achieve in a future period. It allocates resources, sets targets, communicates intent, and provides the benchmark against which actual performance will be evaluated.
2 · Why we budget

The six roles a budget plays.

A budget is rarely just a forecast. The same document is asked to do six different jobs at once — and the tensions between them are the source of most budgetary dysfunction.

01
Planning
Forces management to think systematically about the year ahead — sales, costs, investments, headcount — before the year happens.
Forward-looking
02
Evaluation
Provides the benchmark against which actual results are judged. Without a budget, there is no "favourable" or "unfavourable" — only numbers.
Performance ruler
03
Allocation
Decides how scarce resources — cash, headcount, marketing spend — are split between competing projects, units, and priorities.
Resource scarcity
04
Communication
Sends a signal across the organisation about what leadership cares about. The budget is strategy made legible to everyone who reads a P&L.
Strategic signal
05
Coordination
Synchronises decisions across functions — sales volumes feed production schedules, which feed procurement, which feeds cash. Budgets glue the firm together.
Cross-function
06
Motivation
Sets stretch targets that, when tied to bonuses, push managers to perform. The risk: targets get gamed, or get padded so they're easy to hit.
Incentive design
Why this matters
The six roles often conflict.
A budget that is honest (planning) tends to be conservative — bad for motivation. A budget that is ambitious (motivation) tends to be unrealistic — bad for evaluation. Mature finance functions explicitly separate planning budgets from commitment budgets to manage this tension.
3 · How we control

The seven budgetary control approaches.

Once a budget exists, there is still a choice about how to use it as a control tool. These seven approaches are not mutually exclusive — most firms use a combination — but each has a distinct logic, use-case, and failure mode.

Approach 01 · Fixed (static) budgeting
A single budget set for the year and held constant.
Use case: stable environments where volume is predictable.
Limitation: any deviation in volume produces a "variance" that may simply reflect higher activity rather than poor performance. Useless for fast-moving businesses.
Approach 02 · Flexible budgeting
The budget is recomputed at the actual level of activity.
Use case: any business where volume varies — retail, manufacturing, services.
Limitation: requires you to have correctly identified which costs are variable versus fixed. Misclassify, and the flexed budget is wrong by construction. Foundation for proper variance analysis (module 05).
Approach 03 · Incremental budgeting
"Last year, plus or minus a few percent."
Use case: bureaucracies, public sector, mature businesses with stable cost bases.
Limitation: entrenches whatever was wrong last year. Once a line item exists in the base, it tends to stay forever — what budget veterans call budget inertia.
Approach 04 · Zero-Based Budgeting (ZBB)
Every line item starts at zero and must be justified from scratch.
Use case: turnarounds, cost-cutting programmes, companies under activist pressure.
Limitation: enormously time-consuming. The honest version of ZBB takes months and burns out the finance team — most "ZBB" implementations are really just incremental budgeting with a marketing department.
Approach 05 · Rolling budgets
Always look 12 months ahead — re-forecast quarterly.
Use case: volatile industries, technology, anything where 12-month visibility is fiction.
Limitation: de-emphasises the annual rhythm that many bonus and reporting structures depend on. Requires real discipline — otherwise it becomes "always re-forecasting, never accountable."
Approach 06 · Responsibility-based budgeting
Each responsibility centre owns its own budget.
Use case: divisionalised firms — every cost, revenue, profit and investment centre is given a budget matched to what it actually controls.
Limitation: requires clean cost allocations. When centres share resources (a central marketing function, shared IT), boundaries blur and arguments multiply.
Approach 07 · Activity-Based Budgeting (ABB)
Budget by activity, not by cost centre.
Use case: service firms and manufacturers with high overhead — budgets are built around activities (set-up, inspection, customer onboarding) and the cost drivers behind them.
Limitation: requires an Activity-Based Costing model already in place. Without the underlying activity dictionary, ABB is impossible to implement honestly.
4 · One table to remember

Approaches compared.

A side-by-side reference. The right approach is rarely a single choice — most mature finance functions run a flexible budget for control, an incremental top-down for stability, and ZBB for cost programmes.

Approach Best for Watch out for
Fixed Stable, predictable volume Variances that just reflect volume swings
Flexible Volume-sensitive businesses Cost classification mistakes (FC vs VC)
Incremental Mature, low-change cost bases Inertia — wrongs get baked in forever
ZBB Turnarounds, cost programmes Time burden & "ZBB-in-name-only"
Rolling Volatile industries Loss of annual accountability rhythm
Responsibility-based Divisionalised firms Shared-cost allocations & turf wars
Activity-Based High-overhead service firms Requires existing ABC infrastructure

6 · Test your understanding

Three short questions.

Which of the six roles of budgeting is most likely to be undermined when senior management uses budget targets to set bonus payouts?
When budgets drive bonuses, managers have an incentive to negotiate easy targets — undermining honest planning. The best practice is to separate planning budgets from commitment budgets.
A factory's static budget assumed 10,000 units. Actuals come in at 12,000 units, with proportionally higher variable costs. The reported "unfavourable variance" on variable costs is misleading because…
This is the central case for flexible budgeting: at higher volume, higher variable costs are expected. Flex the budget to 12,000 units before computing the variance.
Which approach starts every line item from zero and requires explicit justification each cycle?
ZBB is the clean-slate approach. ABB starts from activities; rolling re-forecasts; incremental works from last year's base — none of those start at zero.
