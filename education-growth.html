Fairphone (A)

Can a start-up change an industry?

A social enterprise builds the world's first responsibly sourced smartphone — and then runs into the limits of scale.

01 · Executive Summary

A start-up against an industry.

In early 2017, Amsterdam-based Fairphone entered its fourth year with the credentials of a movement: a UN award, a European Business Award for the Environment, B Corp certification, and a forecast for record sales. CEO and founder Bas van Abel had proven that a smartphone could be built around responsible sourcing — starting with the four "conflict minerals" (tin, tungsten, tantalum, gold) that fund violence in the Democratic Republic of Congo.

And yet, twenty-four hours before his first board meeting of the year, Van Abel was walking the Amstel to clear his head. The same year-over-year demand growth that made Fairphone a poster child for ESG had collided with the brute economics of consumer electronics: minimum order quantities, supplier consolidation, and the disappearance of a "piggyback" partner that had let Fairphone access components at sub-industry volumes.

The realities of running an industry-changing business meant we faced continuous challenges that stood in the way of reaching our sales ambitions. Our biggest issue in 2017 can be summed up in one word: production.

04 · The Two Cases

Two strategic decisions,
one underlying problem: scale.

Van Abel arrives at the board meeting carrying two distinct dilemmas. They look unrelated — one is upstream (mining), the other downstream (production) — but both reduce to the same fundamental question: how does a small mission-driven company exert leverage over a supply chain it does not own?

— Case I
Responsible Sourcing at the Limits of Influence
Upstream · Mining & Smelters

Fairphone has solved the four 3TG minerals one at a time through partnership — joining the Conflict-Free Tin Initiative in 2013, Solutions for Hope for tantalum, Fairtrade-certified gold from Peru via Zhaojin Kanfort, and a conflict-free tungsten route through a Rwandan-Austrian partnership.

But each win has required Fairphone to "piggyback" on its suppliers' larger customers to reach the demand threshold that makes a dedicated supply line viable. The gold supply chain alone took two years of work with AT&S and its partners. Mass balance — not closed-pipe traceability — was the compromise required to ship.

The Decision
Where to lead next? — Build a responsible gold supply chain in Africa (highest ESG impact, hardest execution), or pivot to a new precious metal like cobalt (where lithium-ion demand is exploding)?
Is partnership a sustainable model? — Or does Fairphone need to grow large enough to set its own supply-chain rules?
— Case II
LCD Modules, MOQs, and the Disappearing Piggyback
Downstream · Production & Inventory

Months earlier, Fairphone's LCD module supplier closed the factory producing FP2's screens. Van Abel's team redesigned the module — at significant cost — and resumed production by piggybacking on a lead customer ordering similar LCD cells, allowing Fairphone to slot its small volume into a much larger batch.

In mid-2017, that lead customer stopped producing its device. Fairphone's orders go with it. The supplier's MOQ — somewhere between 150,000 and 200,000 units — is far above what Fairphone can absorb. The cash required to buy out remaining inventory would compete directly with operating capital, and unsold modules risk obsolescence.

The Options
Buy out remaining inventory. — Solves supply short-term, exposes balance sheet, obsolescence risk if FP3 specs change.
Negotiate MOQ down for a final batch. — Cheap if it works; relationship-dependent and almost certainly partial.
Find a new piggyback partner. — Repeats the model that just failed; takes time the waiting list does not have.
Retain waiting-list customers through the gap. — A communications problem on top of an operational one.

06 · Tensions

The contradictions Fairphone has to hold.

Mission-driven companies often present their value as monolithic. Fairphone's real architecture is a set of structural trade-offs — none of which has a clean answer.

Mission
Solve the gold supply chain in Africa, where the ESG impact is highest.
Capital
Move to cobalt, where booming EV/battery demand creates leverage Fairphone can ride.
Scale
Stay small to remain ethically uncompromised and movement-credible.
Survival
Grow large enough to dictate MOQs, traceability, and supplier behaviour.
Partnership
Borrow other firms' demand to access ethical supply — fast and capital-light.
Sovereignty
Build dedicated supply — slow, expensive, but resilient when piggybacks disappear.
Closed Pipe
Full traceability from mine to module — provable ESG, narrow material flow.
Mass Balance
Equivalent volumes tracked, not physical units — pragmatic, scalable, harder to market.
07 · Strategic Takeaways

What this case teaches.

Beyond the immediate board decisions, the Fairphone case sits inside a wider question about how mission-driven ventures grow inside industries optimised for scale.

i
Coalition is leverage.
Every Fairphone supply-chain win came from joining or building a multi-party initiative — CFTI, Solutions for Hope, AT&S + partners, Fairtrade. A small firm cannot move a smelter; a coalition of small firms collectively can.
ii
Demand aggregation is the unit of work.
The piggyback model is the dominant tactic — and the dominant single point of failure. When the lead customer leaves, the model collapses. Diversifying piggyback partners is a strategic priority disguised as a procurement chore.
iii
Modularity is a defensive moat.
FP2's modular architecture lengthens product life and lowers e-waste — but it also reduces dependence on any single component supplier. Design is a supply-chain strategy in disguise.
iv
Mission credibility is the demand engine.
FP1 sold out without a retail channel because the story was the product. Operational stumbles threaten the story before they threaten the P&L — and the waiting list is more fragile than it looks.
v
Regulation reshapes geographies overnight.
Dodd-Frank's conflict-mineral provisions collapsed legitimate East African tungsten exports — punishing miners the law intended to protect. Compliance ≠ impact; smart firms can rebuild what the rule broke, and capture both reputation and supply.
vi
Cash is the binding constraint, not demand.
Fairphone's central problem is not "do customers want this?" but "does the balance sheet survive the MOQ?" — a paradox common to capital-light, mission-led growth companies the world over.
08 · Read-Across

Why this case matters here.

The Fairphone case is, structurally, a case about advisory, capital, and operations operating in tension — the same three pillars JC Group is organised around. Van Abel's choices in early 2017 are the choices a holding-company chairman faces: where to allocate scarce capital, which strategic partnerships compound, which operational dependencies break the thesis.

JC Group · Reading Frame

Advisory without execution is theory. Investment without operations is speculation. Fairphone in 2017 is what happens when all three pillars are asked the same question at once — and the answer determines whether the mission survives the scale.
